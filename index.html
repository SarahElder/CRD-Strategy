Vision Executive Summary & Strategic Pillars

Calgary Roller Derby is entering a deliberate transformation. We are shifting from a maintenance-mode organization reliant on heroic individual effort, to a scalable, data-informed league with sustainable systems, empowered leadership, and the capacity to grow. This plan operates across five strategic pillars that reinforce each other. Systems enable growth. Technology enables accountability. Culture enables retention. All of it together enables a thriving house team program and, ultimately, competitive travel teams.

01 ⚙️

Scalable Systems

Every committee documents its processes, builds annual calendars, and operates with enough redundancy that no single person's departure breaks operations. Process audits precede all growth. Volunteer contribution is measured by outcomes, not hours.

02 💡

Technology & Data

Notion becomes our central operating system — for planning, reporting, tracking, and building tools like roster builders and membership databases. A CRM manages space and sponsorship pipelines. Data completeness and dashboard-driven decision-making are core habits, not aspirations.

03 🏛️

Governance & Leadership

The board shifts from maintenance to strategy mode. Bylaws are current. Committee heads are real leaders with coaching, expectations, and strategic plans — not solo operators burning out. Term lengths increase for continuity. Decisions are logged. Institutional memory is protected.

04 📈

Growth & Success

Growth is sequenced deliberately: fix systems first, secure space, then recruit. Target 60 active skaters. KPIs are defined for every function. Sponsorship generates sustainable revenue. Marketing channels are evaluated and prioritized annually.

05 ❤️

Culture & Retention

A thriving league retains as hard as it recruits. The Culture Committee (formerly DEI) owns skater retention, alumni engagement, the end-of-year awards celebration, and the belonging experience. New skaters are onboarded intentionally and connected to the community in their first year.

06 🏟️

Space & Long-Range Vision

Space is the constraint on everything. Year 1 audits and builds the CRM. Year 2 secures additional bookable venues. The long-range vision — a permanent home for derby in Calgary — begins with civic engagement now and matures over 5–10 years.

Foundation Governance, Board Operations & Technology

Governance and technology are prerequisites — not parallel tracks. These initiatives run concurrently with Year 1 committee work and unlock everything else.

📜
Governance

Bylaws & Policy Manual Review

Close the gap between written policy and actual practice. Outdated documents create confusion, inconsistent enforcement, and governance risk.

  • Audit bylaws and policy manual against current operations
  • Identify and close all gaps — update or formally recognize exceptions
  • Review committee head term lengths — extend for continuity and strategic ownership
  • Flag Culture Committee scope update: owns retention, alumni, end-of-year awards
  • Establish annual or bi-annual review cycle
⚙️
Administration

Admin Automation & Google Drive Overhaul

Two Google Drive accounts require clear ownership rules. No document should be owned by an individual — everything critical lives under league email accounts.

  • Define what lives on the BOD Drive vs. League Drive — document and communicate
  • Establish rules: who can own, edit, share, and delete in each drive
  • Migrate any incorrectly placed or individually-owned documents
  • Audit all recurring admin tasks — automate or calendar everything possible
  • Reduce key person risk across all admin functions
Risk: documents owned by departing members can vanish. Ownership must sit with league email accounts, not individuals.
🏛️
Board Operations

Board: Maintenance Mode → Strategy Mode

The board must shift from keeping things running to driving the organization forward. Every board member owns a piece of this plan.

  • Assign board-level ownership to each strategic initiative
  • Establish board reporting rhythms aligned to the governance calendar
  • Distribute institutional knowledge — no board member is a single point of failure
  • Conduct stakeholder review of this plan before distribution to committee heads
  • Build succession planning into every board role
🗂️
Technology

Notion: Central Operating System

Notion is where CRD plans, tracks, reports, and builds tools. One paid account (league email) owns and builds all systems. Everyone else joins free as guests. Rollout is one committee at a time — tied to each committee's process audit, not before.

  • Standard templates: committee planning pages, annual calendars, meeting notes, volunteer deliverable tracking
  • Decision records database: all formal board/committee decisions logged with date, rationale, and owner
  • KPI dashboards: committee heads review their metrics before every meeting — no surprises, no catching up mid-meeting
  • Workload visibility: Notion database shows who is carrying what across committees — enables proactive burnout prevention before it happens
  • Skater pages: individual attendance, volunteer contributions, and training milestone tracking
How we run meetings: Review and update Notion pages live. Log all formal decisions in the decisions records database. Everyone reviews their KPI dashboard before attending — meetings are for decisions, not status updates.
📊
Technology — Data & Tools

Membership Database & Notion-Built Tools

Notion's database layer enables tools that used to require expensive software. Data completeness is a KPI — a database is only as useful as the information in it.

  • Annual Membership Survey: Notion-based, replaces the existing annual survey. Collects full skater info plus strategic questions: plans to return next season (membership forecasting), skill goals, committee interest, availability changes
  • Roster & Line Builder: Pulls from the membership database — current availability, skills, team assignments — to assist coaches building lines for games and practices
  • Travel Planner Templates: Per-tournament pages with hotel room sharing plan, carpool coordination, flight records, and a cost-split calculator for equitable expense sharing
  • Skater Milestone Tracker: Each skater page shows their progression through the training program — skills passed, levels achieved, next milestones
  • KPI: Data Completeness — % of skater profiles fully populated. Measured each season. Target: 100%
🤝
Volunteers

Outcome-Based Volunteer Framework

Replace hours-logged with deliverable-based contribution. Every volunteer has a defined output. This reframes volunteering as value creation — and makes recognition meaningful and specific.

  • Each volunteer role has at least one defined deliverable or KPI
  • Skater pages display volunteer contributions alongside attendance
  • Committee heads report on volunteer outputs in Notion, not just activity counts
  • Workload visibility in Notion helps identify over-loaded volunteers before they burn out
  • Communicate the shift intentionally — frame it as recognition, not surveillance
  • External volunteer management educator brought in Year 1 to support committee heads
🏷️
Membership

Membership Committee: Automate & Expand

Membership currently does admin and basic tracking — with key person risk. Automate the admin, then expand scope to own skater onboarding and, in Year 2–3, alumni engagement.

  • Audit all membership admin tasks — automate or template everything repeatable
  • Document all processes; ensure at least two people can execute each task
  • Own the annual membership survey and database completeness
  • Define and own the new skater onboarding lifecycle (see Year 1)
  • Year 2–3: alumni network management in coordination with Culture Committee

New Skater Experience

Skater Lifecycle & Onboarding Design

Map the full journey from first contact to active contributing member — for both rookies and experienced transfers. Eager new skaters are currently under-utilized; this is a silent retention and culture risk.

  • Map end-to-end lifecycle: discovery → tryout → rookie program → active skater → committee involvement
  • Identify gaps between rookie program completion and full league integration
  • Create a lighter, separate onboarding path for transfers
  • Design intentional touchpoints connecting new skaters to committees in their first year
  • Rookie milestone tracker visible on their Notion skater page
  • Owned by Membership; coordinated with Training and Culture Committee
Rookie Integration Initiatives
  • Buddy System: Every rookie grad is paired with an experienced league skater for their first month of full league practices — a structured, friendly anchor as they navigate the transition from rookie program to the wider league
  • Required League Exposure During Rookie Program: Formalize the expectation that rookies attend a set number of league practices and scrimmages while still in the program — so they know what to expect, start recognizing faces, and feel less like outsiders on graduation day
  • Post-Practice Hangout Culture: Intentionally build the habit of post-practice social time that includes both rookies and league skaters — low-cost, high-impact for belonging and retention. Doesn't need to be formal; just normalized
Rookies are often the most eager people in the league. The goal is that by the time they graduate, they already feel like they belong — not like they're starting over.

Risk — Travel Teams

Charter A Retirement Risk & Cascade Effect

Both travel teams are having successful seasons — but a wave of retirements from Charter A could trigger a cascade that destabilizes both teams simultaneously.

  • The Risk: Charter A loses significant skaters to retirement → Charter B skaters are called up to fill Charter A → Charter B is decimated with no back-feed from house teams to replace them
  • Current Status: Timeline is unclear — this could become critical within months or over 1–2 seasons
  • House team rebuild (this entire plan) is the long-term solution — but won't be complete before the transition hits
  • Monitor retirement intentions actively — informal check-ins with Charter A skaters on future availability
  • Consider whether a merged or restructured travel program makes sense in a transitional period
  • Explore whether retiring skaters can transition to officiating, coaching, or mentoring roles to maintain continuity
Contingency: if significant retirements are confirmed before Year 3, move travel team restructuring into Year 2 planning. The goal is a single, healthy, sustainable travel program — not two underpopulated teams.

Funding Strategy

Grant Strategy: Types, Scale & Approach

Grants are a real funding pathway — but require targeted effort and realistic expectations. The right grants for CRD depend on what we're applying for: operations, events, space development, or community programs.

Grant Categories & Realistic Scale
  • City of Calgary Community Grants — Community Vitality and Recreation grants for sport/community organizations. Typically $2,000–$25,000. Accessible, good starting point. Annual applications.
  • Alberta Sport Connection / Sport Alberta — Provincial funding for amateur sport development, events, and capacity building. Range: $5,000–$50,000 depending on program. Requires demonstrated sport development impact.
  • Canadian Heritage / Sport Canada — Federal funding for national/regional sport development, hosting, and inclusion programs (especially Pride-aligned initiatives). $10,000–$100,000+ but competitive and requires strong reporting infrastructure.
  • Alberta Women's Sport Fund / Jumpstart (Canadian Tire) — Targeted at women's and girls' sport participation. $5,000–$30,000. CRD's demographics align well.
  • Infrastructure / Facility Grants — For permanent space: Alberta Recreation Facility grants, Canada Community Building Fund (via municipalities). These are large ($100,000+) but require municipal co-application and are multi-year processes.
  • LGBTQ+ / Inclusion Grants — Pride-aligned funders (e.g. Calgary Foundation, Rainbow Resource Centre funding streams). $2,000–$15,000. Align with Pride games and Culture Committee mandate.
Realistic Net Returns
  • Grant writers (professional or academic) typically take 10–20% of awarded funds as a fee, or charge flat hourly rates ($50–$150/hr)
  • Year 1–2: focus on smaller, accessible municipal and provincial grants — lower barrier, build a track record
  • Year 3+: use that track record to pursue larger provincial and federal grants
  • Facility grants require a municipal partner — this is why city committee engagement starts in Year 2
  • Academic partnership (MBA capstone) is the most cost-effective way to do the heavy lifting on grant research and first-draft applications
Realistic expectation: $10,000–$40,000/year from smaller grants once a track record is established. Facility grants are transformational but 5–10 year horizon. Don't rely on grants as operating revenue — treat them as capital and program funds.

Process Stakeholder Engagement Map

Before this plan becomes official policy, consult the right people in the right order. Who gets input, who gets a preview, and who gets informed — matters. Sequence: Board → Key Leaders → Committee Heads / Juniors / Zebra → General League.

Stage 1 — Co-Create
Board of Directors
Review, challenge, and commit to the plan. Must own it before anyone else sees it. This is the group that needs to shift from maintenance to strategy mode.
Stage 2 — Input
Key Senior Leaders
Experienced committee heads and trusted voices whose buy-in is critical. Consult before committee-wide distribution. Their perspective improves the plan and their support accelerates adoption.
Stage 3 — Engaged
All Committee Heads
Receive the finalized plan. Asked to build their own committee-level plans in response. Given clear context on board expectations and what support they will receive.
Stage 3 — Parallel
Junior Leaders & Team Zebra
Receive the plan alongside committee heads. Asked to use it as a framework for their own sub-plans. Ownership sits entirely with them.
Stage 4 — Informed
General League Membership
Receive a summary of the vision and Year 1 priorities. Transparency builds trust and engagement. Operational detail stays internal.
